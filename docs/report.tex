% ── Abstract ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
We present a structured five-stage ablation study of reward shaping and
architectural design in a shared-parameter Multi-Agent Proximal Policy
Optimisation (MAPPO) system, evaluated on the Knights-Archers-Zombies (KAZ)
cooperative environment from PettingZoo. Starting from a bare kill-reward
baseline (\gameone: \emph{Greedy Soldier}), we progressively stack ammo
restrictions (\gametwo), stamina penalties (\gamethree), 60/40 team-health
rewards with entity-level self-attention (\gamefour), and finally Gaussian
observation fog with GRU temporal memory (\gamefive: \emph{Fire Discipline}).
Our central finding is the diagnosis and resolution of \emph{deterministic
passivity}---a policy collapse phenomenon where agents trained with strong
penalties learn to avoid all action under a greedy (argmax) policy. In a V2
design iteration, we add a configurable death penalty ($-2.0$ per death) to
test whether a survival incentive alone fixes passivity. It does not: MLP
agents (G2--G3) remain fully passive. Only \gamefive{}'s GRU temporal memory
converts the survival signal into active behaviour, confirming that
architectural capacity---not reward magnitude---is the bottleneck. All code,
checkpoints, and visualisations are available in the project repository.
\end{abstract}

% ── 1. Introduction ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) presents unique
challenges beyond single-agent RL: agents must coordinate emergent behaviour
without explicit communication, manage shared resources, and remain robust to
partial observability. Reward shaping is a powerful but double-edged tool---it
can guide agents toward desired behaviours while inadvertently suppressing
beneficial exploration or inducing unintended policy collapse
\cite{ng1999policy}.

This work asks a focused question: \emph{how does the progressive addition of
environmental constraints and architectural upgrades shape the emergent
behaviour of MAPPO agents?} We operationalise this through a five-game
ablation where each game adds exactly one new element, enabling clean
attribution of behavioural changes.

The Knights-Archers-Zombies environment \cite{terry2021pettingzoo} provides an
ideal testbed: four cooperative agents (2 knights, 2 archers) must eliminate
zombies while managing team health. The combination of close-range melee and
ranged combat creates natural specialisation pressure, while the scoring
function based on kills introduces a tension between individual efficiency and
team preservation.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item A five-stage ablation demonstrating how constraint stacking drives
    \emph{behavioural collapse} then \emph{recovery} in MAPPO agents.
  \item Diagnosis of \emph{deterministic passivity}---a novel failure mode
    where the stochastic policy learns to be aggressive but the deterministic
    policy is passive---and its resolution via GRU recurrence.
  \item An architectural progression from MLP to entity-attention to
    attention+GRU, with empirical evidence that temporal memory is the key
    ingredient for resolving passivity under partial observability.
  \item Gradient-based saliency and attention weight visualisations
    comparing where \gameone{} (MLP) and \gamefive{} (Attn+GRU) focus.
\end{enumerate}

% ── 2. Background ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Background \& Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\paragraph{MAPPO.} \citet{yu2022surprising} showed that Proximal Policy
Optimisation \cite{schulman2017proximal} with shared parameters achieves
competitive performance in cooperative MARL without the need for centralised
critics or communication channels. We follow their shared-parameter formulation
where all agents share a single actor-critic network.

\paragraph{Reward shaping.} \citet{ng1999policy} prove that potential-based
reward shaping preserves the optimal policy under certain conditions. However,
non-potential-based shaping (e.g.\ stamina penalties) can distort the optimal
policy. Our ablation deliberately explores this distortion as a feature,
demonstrating how over-constraining without architectural support induces
passivity.

\paragraph{Attention in MARL.} \citet{iqbal2019actor} introduced
actor-attention-critic, using multi-head attention to dynamically weight agent
contributions. We apply entity-level self-attention within each agent's
observation (over the 27 entity slots) rather than across agents, providing
permutation-invariant feature extraction.

\paragraph{Recurrent MARL.} \citet{hausknecht2015deep} demonstrated that GRU
hidden states allow policies to integrate information across time steps in
partially observable environments. We add a GRUCell after the attention encoder
to integrate noisy observations from Gaussian fog across timesteps.

\paragraph{MADDPG \& counterfactual baselines.} \citet{lowe2017multi} and
\citet{foerster2018counterfactual} use centralised training / decentralised
execution (CTDE). We deliberately maintain a fully decentralised execution
approach (shared parameters, local observations), focusing on emergent
cooperation rather than centralised credit assignment.

% ── 3. Environment ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Environment \& Problem Setup}
\label{sec:env}

\subsection{Knights-Archers-Zombies (KAZ)}

KAZ \cite{terry2021pettingzoo} is a parallel multi-agent environment with 4
agents: 2 knights (melee) and 2 archers (ranged). Zombies spawn from the top
of the screen and must be eliminated before reaching the bottom. Each agent
observes an entity-centric vector of $27 \times 5 = 135$ features, encoding
the type, health, $x$, $y$, and orientation of the 27 nearest entities (self,
teammates, zombies, arrows). The action space has 6 discrete actions:
\{move-forward, move-backward, rotate-left, rotate-right, attack, no-op\}.

\subsection{KAZWrapper: Game-Level Modifiers}

We implement a \texttt{KAZWrapper} that wraps PettingZoo's parallel API and
applies game-level modifications:

The total shaped reward is:
\begin{equation}
r_i = w_s \cdot r_\text{aggression}^{(i)} + w_t \cdot \bar{r}_\text{team}^{(-i)} - d \cdot \mathbf{1}[\text{agent}_i \text{ dies}]
\label{eq:reward}
\end{equation}
where $w_s = 0.6$, $w_t = 0.4$ (Games 4--5), $d$ is the death penalty (2.0 in
V2, 0.0 in V1), and $\bar{r}_\text{team}^{(-i)}$ is the mean reward of agent
$i$'s teammates. For Games 1--3, $w_s = 1$, $w_t = 0$.

% ── 4. Methods ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{MAPPO with Shared Parameters}

All agents share a single \texttt{MAPPONet} actor-critic backbone. Each agent
independently observes its local observation $o_i \in \mathbb{R}^{135}$,
produces a discrete action distribution $\pi_\theta(a_i \mid o_i)$, and a
scalar value estimate $V_\phi(o_i)$. We use the clipped PPO objective:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_\text{PPO} = \mathbb{E}_t\left[\min\left(
  r_t(\theta)\hat{A}_t,\;
  \text{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon)\hat{A}_t
\right)\right]
\end{equation}
with entropy bonus $\mathcal{L}_\text{ent} = \beta H[\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid o_t)]$
and entropy floor: $\beta \leftarrow 2\beta$ if $H < H_\text{min}$.

\subsection{Architecture Evolution}

\paragraph{Games 1--3: MLP Backbone.}
A 3-layer MLP: $\text{Linear}(135, 256) \to \text{ReLU} \to
\text{Linear}(256, 256) \to \text{ReLU} \to \text{Linear}(256, 256)$.
Shared actor/critic heads sit on top. Total: $\approx$200K parameters.

\paragraph{Game 4: Entity Self-Attention (MLP + Attn).}
The observation is reshaped to $(27, 5)$ entity slots. An
\texttt{EntityAttentionEncoder} projects each slot to 256-d, applies 4-head
self-attention with masking of empty slots, then mean-pools attended features.
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{A}|}\sum_{i \in \mathcal{A}} \text{Attn}(E(e_i), E(\mathbf{e}), E(\mathbf{e}))
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{A}$ is the set of non-empty entity slots and $E$ is the entity
embedding. Total: $\approx$467K parameters.

\paragraph{Game 5: Attention + GRU (attention\_gru).}
A GRUCell$(256, 256)$ is appended after the attention pooling:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}_t = \text{GRUCell}(\mathbf{z}_t, \mathbf{h}_{t-1}), \quad \mathbf{h}_0 = \mathbf{0}
\end{equation}
Hidden states are maintained per-agent across rollout steps and reset at
episode boundaries. Transfer learning initialises 26/26 compatible tensors from
the \gamefour{} checkpoint. Total: 825,481 parameters.

% ── 5. Experiments ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Game 1 --- The Greedy Soldier}
\textit{Constraint: Kill reward only. Architecture: MLP.}

The baseline agent maximises kills without constraint. With infinite ammo and
no resource costs, the agent learns to continuously attack, achieving a raw
kill density of $\mathbf{0.01051}$ kills/step (V2)---the highest across all
games and essentially unchanged from V1 ($0.01089$). The death penalty ($d=2.0$)
has negligible impact on \gameone{} because agents that fight aggressively
accumulate enough kill reward to offset the penalty. Mean return drops from
$5.80$ (V1) to $3.98$ (V2) due to the penalty itself, not behavioural change.

\subsection{Game 2 --- The Risk Avoider}
\textit{Constraint: +Ammo limit (15 arrows/episode). Architecture: MLP.}

Ammo scarcity creates a negative feedback loop: each missed shot costs the
archer both an arrow and a future kill opportunity. The agent learns to \emph{
not shoot} by default, reducing raw kill density by 71\% to $0.00308$ (V2).
The death penalty compounds the avoidance behaviour: agents pay $-2.0$ for
dying but lack the representational capacity to convert ``don't die'' into
``fight back.'' Episode length drops to 203 steps as passive agents are overrun.

\subsection{Game 3 --- The Fully Passive}
\textit{Constraint: +Stamina decay ($c_\text{move}=0.01$, $c_\text{atk}=0.05$). Architecture: MLP.}

Stamina penalties on movement and attacks compound the risk-avoidance
behaviour. Even with death penalty $d=2.0$, the MLP policy collapses to
near-complete passivity: raw kill density $0.00297$ (V2), a 72\% reduction
from \gameone{}. Under deterministic evaluation, raw kills are exactly $0.00$
across all 10 episodes---the phenomenon we term \emph{deterministic passivity}.
\textbf{This is the key V2 finding for G3: death penalty alone does not fix
passivity.} The MLP lacks the capacity to translate a survival incentive into
aggressive action.

\subsection{Game 4 --- The Recovering Cooperator}
\textit{Constraint: +60/40 team-health reward. Architecture: MLP + Entity Attention.}

The team-health component introduces a cooperative signal: agents now benefit
from teammates' kills. The entity self-attention enables dynamic weighting of
nearby entities. Kill density is $0.00298$ (V2) with preservation signals
emerging ($\bar{r}_\text{pres} = 0.575$). However, \emph{deterministic
passivity persists}: deterministic raw kills are $0.00$ per episode---identical
to \gamethree. Death penalty + attention is insufficient without temporal
memory.

\subsection{Game 5 --- Fire Discipline}
\textit{Constraint: +Gaussian fog ($\sigma=0.3$). Architecture: MLP + Attention + GRU.}

The GRU hidden state allows agents to integrate noisy observations across
timesteps, building a temporal consensus about entity locations. Transfer
learning from \gamefour{} provides warm-start weights. \gamefive{} is the
only game where death penalty produces a \emph{positive} effect: V2 raw kill
density $0.00373$ (+25\% vs V1's $0.00299$), the highest of any constrained
game. Deterministic evaluation shows $0.33$ raw kills/episode (vs $0.00$ for
G2--G4), with preservation at $+0.325$. The GRU converts the survival signal
into active behaviour that the MLP cannot.

\subsection{Diagnosing Deterministic Passivity}

Deterministic passivity occurs when the entropy of the stochastic policy is
distributed over many actions, but the \emph{mode} (argmax) consistently
selects a low-reward non-attack action. This is distinct from the agent
``giving up''---the stochastic policy still produces attacks with non-trivial
probability---but the deterministic policy (as used in deployment) is
completely passive.

Root cause: strong penalty signals (ammo waste, stamina cost) shift the
reward gradient such that the policy mass around attack actions is lower than
around no-op/movement. Under softmax with temperature $T=1$, the most probable
action is often \textit{no-op}, even though the expected value of attacking
may be positive when weighted across trajectories.

\paragraph{V2 death penalty experiment.} Adding $d=2.0$ death penalty was
hypothesised to fix this by making ``die passively'' (net $-2.0$) worse than
``kill and survive'' (net $+1.0$). Result: \textbf{MLP agents still collapse.}
G3 deterministic raw kills remain $0.00$ per episode. The MLP lacks the
representational capacity to translate a survival incentive into aggressive
action under constraint pressure.

\gamefive{}'s resolution: the GRU hidden state accumulates temporal evidence
about entity positions across noisy observations. After 2--5 timesteps of
consistent zombie presence in the vicinity, the hidden state encodes enough
certainty that the policy mode shifts to \textit{attack}. Crucially, the death
penalty \emph{does} help G5: V2 raw kill density $0.00373$ (+25\% vs V1),
suggesting that the GRU provides the capacity to \emph{use} the survival
signal that MLP models ignore.

% ── 7. Explainability ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Explainability}
\label{sec:explain}

\subsection{G1: Gradient-Based Saliency}

For the MLP policy (\gameone), we compute input gradient saliency: the absolute
partial derivative of the selected action logit with respect to each input
feature, averaged over entity slots:
\begin{equation}
s_j = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \left\|\frac{\partial \log \pi(a^*_t \mid o_t)}{\partial \mathbf{e}_j}\right\|_1 \cdot \mathbf{1}[\mathbf{e}_j \neq \mathbf{0}]
\end{equation}
The MLP's saliency concentrates heavily on a small number of entity slots
(Figure~\ref{fig:saliency}, left), consistent with nearest-enemy tunnel
vision---the agent attends to the single most threatening entity and largely
ignores teammates.

\subsection{G5: Self-Attention Weight Visualisation}

For \gamefive{}, we capture the $27 \times 27$ self-attention weight matrix
$\mathbf{W}_\text{attn}$ from the \texttt{EntityAttentionEncoder} via a
registered forward hook, averaged over 200 steps. Column-wise summation reveals
which entity slots are most \emph{attended to} across all query positions.

The attention distribution (Figure~\ref{fig:saliency}, centre and right) is
notably more distributed than G1's saliency, spanning teammate slots as well as
zombie slots. The attention matrix heatmap (right) shows structured off-diagonal
patterns indicating cross-entity reasoning rather than self-attending only.
Attention sum of 27.0 (one per entity row, each softmax row summing to 1)
confirms well-formed attention weights.

% ── 8. Discussion ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{The Constraint Trap.} Our results demonstrate a fundamental tension
in reward shaping for MARL: each constraint individually motivates a
desirable safety behaviour (ammo conservation, energy management), but their
cumulative effect can suppress the very aggression that makes the agent useful.
Without architectural support, the policy finds the locally safe solution: do
nothing. The V2 death penalty experiment sharpens this finding: even an
explicit survival incentive ($d=2.0$) cannot overcome the trap without
architectural capacity to act on it.

\paragraph{Architecture is not enough---but reward helps with the right
architecture.} Entity attention in \gamefour{} improves feature extraction but
cannot resolve deterministic passivity. However, the death penalty \emph{does}
amplify G5's GRU-enabled aggression (+25\% raw kill density). This suggests
that reward signals and architecture are \emph{complementary}: neither is
sufficient alone, but together they produce the strongest constrained-game
performance.

\paragraph{Transfer learning efficiency.} Loading 26 compatible weight tensors
from \gamefour{} to \gamefive{} (excluding the GRU parameters) significantly
accelerated G5 training. The entity embedding and attention layers transferred
cleanly, suggesting that entity-level representations are robust to the
addition of Gaussian observation noise.

\paragraph{Observation dimension discrepancy.} G1 was trained on an environment
producing 110-dimensional observations (22 entity slots) while G4/G5 used 135
dimensions (27 entity slots). This divergence, likely caused by different
\texttt{max\_cycles} or wrapper parameter defaults between training runs,
prevents direct cross-environment evaluation but does not affect within-game
validity.

% ── 9. Conclusion ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have demonstrated that progressive reward shaping drives MAPPO agents
through a predictable behavioural trajectory: Greedy Soldier $\to$ Risk
Avoider $\to$ Fully Passive $\to$ Recovering Cooperator $\to$ Fire Discipline.
The critical transition from Fully Passive to Fire Discipline requires not just
better rewards but better architecture: specifically, GRU temporal memory to
integrate noisy observations into committed action decisions.

The key finding is the identification and resolution of \emph{deterministic
passivity}, a previously under-described failure mode in constrained cooperative
MARL. The V2 death penalty experiment provides a clean ablation: the same
survival incentive ($d=2.0$) fails in MLP models (G2--G3: zero deterministic
kills) but succeeds in the GRU model (G5: $0.33$ kills/episode, $+25\%$ raw
kill density vs V1). This confirms that architectural capacity is the
bottleneck, and that reward shaping and architecture are complementary tools.

\paragraph{Future Work.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \emph{Cross-game evaluation}: standardise all games to the same
    observation space to enable direct policy transfer evaluation.
  \item \emph{Communication channels}: test whether explicit inter-agent
    messaging can replace temporal memory as a passivity resolver.
  \item \emph{Centralised training}: compare decentralised MAPPO against
    MADDPG \cite{lowe2017multi} or QMIX under the same constraint stack.
  \item \emph{Adaptive entropy floor}: learn the entropy coefficient rather
    than using a fixed doubling heuristic.
  \item \emph{Curriculum fog}: gradually increase fog sigma during training
    rather than applying full $\sigma=0.3$ from step 0.
\end{itemize}
