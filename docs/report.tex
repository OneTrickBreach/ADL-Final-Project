\title{\textbf{Structured Teamwork in Multi-Agent PPO:\\
A Seven-Game Study of Coordination Primitives on Knights-Archers-Zombies}}
\author[1]{ADLProject2 (V3 Overhaul)}
\affil[1]{Advanced Deep Learning, CS7180 2025--2026}
\date{\today}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We study cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning on PettingZoo's
Knights-Archers-Zombies (KAZ) environment through a controlled seven-game
ablation that isolates the contribution of four coordination primitives:
(i) ammo and stamina discipline, (ii) role assignment (forward knight vs.\
end-zone patrol knight), (iii) hard target locks with cross-role skip
rules, and (iv) a pragmatic archer override that violates locks when a
zombie is projected to cross the end zone before a knight can intercept.
Two games (\gz{} through \gb{}) are evaluated with scripted heuristics to
establish ceiling and floor; four games (\gii--\gv{}) are trained with
shared-parameter MAPPO using entity self-attention (\gii{}) or
attention + GRU (\giii--\gv{}) with transfer-learning between adjacent
games. All games share identical episode length, spawn rates, and
evaluation seeds so that score differences isolate the primitive. Our
central finding is that \emph{role assignment is the single most
impactful coordination primitive}: adding forward/patrol roles with
temporal memory (\giii{}) improves stochastic score by \textbf{+96\%}
over the learned ammo-discipline baseline (\gii{}) with no extra reward
engineering. Conversely, the tighter coordination rules introduced in
\giv{} and \gv{} (lock radii, cross-role skip, pragmatic override)
produce a measurable \emph{knight passivity} regime in which archers
perform nearly all eliminations. We argue this is a clean negative
result: the ablation lets us attribute the loss to a specific primitive
and motivates the remediations in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Cooperative MARL presents challenges beyond single-agent RL: agents must
coordinate without explicit communication, share limited resources, and
remain robust to partial observability. Prior work on KAZ and similar
benchmarks typically explores either \emph{reward shaping} or
\emph{architectural upgrades} in isolation.

This work takes a complementary route. Rather than asking which reward
shaping mix is best, we ask: \emph{how much cooperative performance can
be recovered by building structure into the environment itself?} We
define four progressively strict coordination primitives, fix every other
variable (episode length, spawn rate, architecture family, evaluation
seeds), and measure how each primitive affects score and per-role
attack/kill distributions.

\paragraph{Relationship to prior iterations.} An earlier iteration of
this project (V1/V2, preserved on the \texttt{main} and
\texttt{v2-death-penalty} branches) studied \emph{deterministic
passivity}---a failure mode where stacking penalty-based constraints
makes the stochastic policy fire while the argmax policy remains inert.
V3 sets aside that narrative in favour of a positive-reinforcement
progression and reuses only the MAPPO network family and the evaluation
harness.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item A seven-game ablation (\gz--\gv{}) that isolates ammo discipline,
    role assignment, target locks, and pragmatic override under fixed
    spawn rates and seeds.
  \item A seed-paired evaluation protocol (identical zombie spawn pattern
    across games) that enables direct attribution of score deltas to
    primitives.
  \item Quantitative evidence that role assignment is the most impactful
    primitive (\gii{}$\to$\giii{}: +96\% stochastic score; +200\%
    deterministic score) and that tighter lock rules can induce a
    role-specific passivity pathology.
  \item A training-free heuristic baseline covering three ammo regimes,
    showing that the best learned policy (\giii{}) closes less than
    half the gap to the unconstrained heuristic ceiling (\gz{}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:bg}

\paragraph{MAPPO.} \citet{yu2022surprising} showed that Proximal Policy
Optimisation \cite{schulman2017proximal} with shared parameters is a
competitive baseline for cooperative MARL without centralised critics or
inter-agent communication. We follow the shared-parameter formulation.

\paragraph{Reward shaping.} \citet{ng1999policy} prove that potential-based
shaping preserves the optimal policy; non-potential shaping can distort
it. V3 uses minimal shaping and instead moves coordination into the
wrapper, letting us study structure-vs-shaping in a controlled setting.

\paragraph{Attention and recurrence.} \citet{iqbal2019actor} introduced
actor-attention-critic; \citet{vaswani2017attention}'s self-attention
underpins our entity encoder, which operates over the per-agent
\((27, 5)\) entity state. Recurrence via GRU
\cite{cho2014learning, hausknecht2015deep} integrates role and lock
features across the 450-step episode.

\paragraph{CTDE baselines.} MADDPG \cite{lowe2017multi} and COMA
\cite{foerster2018counterfactual} explicitly route gradient information
between agents at training time. We intentionally stay decentralised so
that the coordination signal flows through structural primitives.

\paragraph{Environment.} KAZ is distributed with PettingZoo Butterfly
\cite{terry2021pettingzoo}. It provides a permutation-invariant
\((27, 5)\) entity vector per agent and a discrete 6-way action space
(forward, backward, rotate CCW, rotate CW, attack, no-op).

\section{Environment and Problem Setup}
\label{sec:env}

\subsection{KAZ v10}

Four agents (2 archers, 2 knights) face zombies spawning at the top of a
\(1280 \times 720\)-pixel arena. A \emph{failure} is a zombie that reaches
the bottom. Episodes last 30 s at 15 FPS (450 steps). We report
\(\text{score} = \text{kills} - \text{failures}\) per episode.

\subsection{V3 wrapper}

\texttt{KAZWrapperV3} takes a \texttt{game\_level} string and configures:
ammo pools (off / global / individual), knight stamina pool,
archer-immobile mode (\ga/\gb{} only), fixed role assignment, target
lock acquisition, a \gv{} pragmatic-override rule, and a shared failure
penalty. A single configuration table drives all seven games.

\subsection{Reward function}

Base kill reward is KAZ's built-in \(+1\) per elimination by the agent.
V3 adds three auxiliary terms:
\begin{equation}
r_i = r^{\text{kill}}_i
    + b_r \cdot \mathbf{1}_{\text{roles\_on}} \cdot \phi_r(i)
    + b_\ell \cdot \mathbf{1}_{\text{lock\_on}} \cdot \phi_\ell(i)
    - p_f \cdot n_f^{(t)}
    - p_m \cdot \mathbf{1}_{\text{invalid}}(a_i)
\label{eq:reward}
\end{equation}
with \(b_r = 0.001,\; b_\ell = 0.002,\; p_f = 1.0,\; p_m = 0.05\).
\(\phi_r(i) = \mathbf{1}\{\text{agent $i$ is in its assigned zone}\}\),
\(\phi_\ell(i) = \mathbf{1}\) iff \(i\) attacked while holding a valid
lock. \(n_f^{(t)}\) is the number of zombies that crossed this step
(shared across living agents).

\subsection{Observation augmentation}

Each agent's KAZ observation (shape \((27, 5)\)) is appended with a 5-dim
extras row encoding \{role\_id, in\_end\_zone, ammo\_frac,
stamina\_frac, has\_lock\}, giving a flat observation of 140 dimensions
identical across all seven games. This preserves checkpoint compatibility
and lets transfer-learning across \gii--\gv{} copy 22+ / 32 tensors.

\subsection{Action masking}

V3 supports both \emph{soft} (penalty) and \emph{hard} (forced no-op)
masking for invalid actions. All experiments use soft masking to keep
gradients informative; a direction-aware hard mask additionally prevents
patrol-knight boundary crossings when \texttt{lock\_on} is set.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Shared-parameter MAPPO with extras}

All four agents share a single actor-critic network. We use the clipped
PPO objective with GAE. The encoder splits the 140-d observation into
the KAZ entity block (\(27 \times 5\)) and the 5-d extras:
\[
\mathbf{z}_t = \underbrace{\mathrm{Pool}(\mathrm{MHA}(E(\mathrm{obs}_t)))}_{\text{entity attention}}
\;+\; W_{\text{extra}}\,\mathrm{extras}_t
\]
with a mean pool over non-padded entity slots. For \giii--\gv{} we pass
\(\mathbf{z}_t\) through a GRUCell,
\(\mathbf{h}_t = \mathrm{GRUCell}(\mathbf{z}_t, \mathbf{h}_{t-1})\),
with per-agent hidden state reset at episode boundaries.

\paragraph{Transfer learning.} \giii{} transfers 28 / 32 tensors from
\gii{} (the 4 GRU parameter tensors are new); \giv{} and \gv{} each
transfer all 32 / 32 from their predecessor.

\subsection{Heuristic baselines}

A scripted policy queries the wrapper for the nearest zombie, aims,
and fires. Knights walk forward when aligned and attack within 60 px.
Ammo and stamina are respected. The heuristic never uses the policy
network, giving a training-free ceiling (\gz{}) and two constrained
floors (\ga/\gb{}). The ammo pool mode (global vs individual) for
\gii--\gv{} is picked automatically by comparing \ga{} and \gb{}
scores.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Roles give the biggest lift}

Adding role assignment + GRU (\giii{}) raises stochastic score from
\(2.40 \to 4.70\) (+96\%) and doubles the deterministic score. No
reward magnitude was changed between \gii{} and \giii{}: the only
differences were (i) the patrol/forward role\_id feature, (ii) the tiny
role bonus \(b_r = 10^{-3}\), and (iii) the addition of a GRUCell.

Why roles help is intuitive in hindsight. In \gii{}, both knights chase
the nearest zombie; their attack budget is spent on overlapping targets
(56.3 attacks/episode shared, converting to 0.1 kills). In \giii{} the
patrol knight is anchored to the end zone and only attacks zombies that
arrive there, while the forward knight moves freely. Attack budget drops
to 28.9/episode and archer kills rise to 4.4.

\subsection{Locks cause knight passivity}

\giv{} and \gv{} lower the learned score despite transferring from
\giii{} and continuing to train for 1.5--2M more steps. Inspection of
Table~\ref{tab:kills} tells the story: knights attack 0.2--0.5
times/episode, vs 28.9 in \giii{}. The 15\%-width lock radius
(\(192\) px) combined with the patrol constraint means a knight only
sees one or two lockable targets per episode.

This is not a negative result in the colloquial sense: we \emph{learned}
that the specific lock radius we picked is too tight. The experiment
isolates the loss to one primitive, and the remediation path is clear
(Section~\ref{sec:future}).

\subsection{Reversing V1/V2's deterministic passivity}

V1/V2 reported that stacking penalty constraints made the argmax
(deterministic) policy nearly inert while the stochastic policy still
fired. In V3 the opposite is observed: deterministic scores
\emph{increase} along the progression (\gii{} \(0.80 \to\) \gv{}
\(2.60\)). Our interpretation is that the structural primitives
(especially roles) give the policy a scaffolding that the argmax mode
can commit to, whereas penalty stacking makes every action look dangerous
at test time.

\subsection{Zero failures throughout}

Across all 70 evaluation episodes, \(n_f = 0\). The failure penalty
\(p_f = 1.0\) therefore never fires during evaluation, which means the
reward shaping term is effectively unused at test time. This is expected
(default spawn rate is easy relative to 4 agents with stamina pools) but
it also means we cannot claim that our agents have learned to defend
\emph{under pressure}; they have learned to coordinate at a pressure
level where failure is cheap.

\subsection{Ammo mode tie}

\ga{} and \gb{} produced identical scores to two decimal places
(\(3.70 \pm 1.19\)). The orchestration script picks ``global'' by a
deterministic \(\ge\) tiebreak, so \gii--\gv{} are trained under a
shared 30-arrow pool. A separate run under individual ammo would be a
clean follow-up experiment.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:future}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Lock radius sweep.} Scan lock radius in
  \(\{0.15, 0.20, 0.25, 0.30\} \cdot W\) to locate the transition point
  at which knights stop being passive.
\item \textbf{Spawn-rate stress test.} Rerun the ablation at spawn rates
  \(0.7\times, 1.4\times, 2.0\times\) default; this activates the
  failure penalty and lets us observe degradation curves per primitive.
\item \textbf{Per-role heads.} Split the shared policy into per-role
  heads (shared trunk, two heads for forward/patrol knight, one for
  archer). Might remove the need for the role\_id observation feature
  and reduce interference.
\item \textbf{Learned pragmatic override.} Replace the deterministic
  G5 override rule with a learned gating policy. Our current
  implementation hard-codes the cross-vs-intercept calculation;
  letting the archer's policy learn when to override may reveal richer
  coordination patterns.
\item \textbf{Individual-ammo ablation.} Run \gii--\gv{} under
  individual ammo to test sensitivity to the \ga/\gb{} tiebreak.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

We presented a seven-game ablation on PettingZoo KAZ that isolates four
coordination primitives and measures their individual contribution to
score, per-class kills, and attack budgets. Role assignment is the
single biggest structural win (+96\% over a constrained learned
baseline); tighter locks were harmful at the radius we chose; and
deterministic scores improve along the progression, reversing V1/V2's
deterministic-passivity regime.

The full implementation, training pipeline, evaluation harness, and all
artifacts are available in the project repository
(\texttt{v3-overhaul} branch). The ablation protocol---identical seeds
across games, a soft-mask wrapper, and seed-paired stochastic +
deterministic evaluations---is designed to be easy to extend to additional
primitives without invalidating previously trained checkpoints.

\section{V3.1 Addendum: Detector Rewrite \& Pressure Retune}
\label{sec:v3_1}

\paragraph{Motivation.}
Post-submission, a pre-flight of a planned V3.1 parameter sweep revealed
a bug in the V3 failure detector and a hyperparameter regime where the
failure penalty was effectively inert. Specifically: (i) the V3 detector
counted zombies that \emph{disappeared from the zombie list}, gated on
\texttt{any\_kill}, but in KAZ a crossed zombie stays in the list (its
removal happens only via kill), so the detector fired only on ambiguous
multi-disappearance frames; (ii) the default pressure
(\texttt{spawn\_rate=20}, \texttt{max\_zombies=10}) combined with the
heuristic aggression of G0 meant that even random play produced zero
crosses in most episodes. The net effect was Section~\ref{sec:discussion}
\S4.4's ``zero failures throughout''---a property of the instrument,
not the policy.

\paragraph{Fix.}
V3.1 rewrites the detector and retunes six pressure parameters; no
changes to reward composition, model architecture, or curriculum.
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Detector} (\texttt{src/wrappers/kaz\_wrapper\_v3.py}):
line-crossing is counted when a zombie's \texttt{rect.centery} $\ge$
\texttt{SCREEN\_H} $-$ \texttt{ZOMBIE\_Y\_SPEED} (with a per-\texttt{id}
dedup set); agent-breach is counted via the sprite-list delta
\texttt{prev\_alive\_count $-$ cur\_alive}. An optional
episode-continuation override flips \texttt{raw.run = True} when
\texttt{cur\_alive > 0}, so multiple line-crosses can be counted in one
rollout.
\item \textbf{Pressure defaults:} \texttt{spawn\_rate} 20$\to$8,
\texttt{max\_zombies} 10$\to$20, lock radius 0.15$\to$0.25 $W$, ammo
30/15$\to$60/30, knight stamina 100$\to$150.
\item \textbf{Risk~\#3 cap:} reward term clamped at
\texttt{min(failures, 3)} per step to prevent catastrophic advantage
spikes while the raw counter is left unclamped for metrics.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Pre-flight gates.} All four passed on the retuned wrapper
before retraining: $g1a$ \texttt{failures\_mean} = 3.33 ($\ge 2$);
$g0$ \texttt{failures\_mean} = 3.00 ($> 0$, $\le 2 \times$ $g0$ kills =
36.00); all three $g1a$ pre-flight episodes had \texttt{ep\_len}
$\in \{151, 175, 173\}$, i.e.\ $> 150$.

$\ge 1$ learned game with failures $> 0$; \giii{} $\ge$ \gii{}; \giv{} $\ge$
\giii{}; \gv{} $\ge$ \giv{}.

\paragraph{Which V3 claims survive, which are revised.}

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Roles give a lift (V3~\S5.1)}: still true.
\giii{} $-$ \gii{} $=$ +2.30 points stochastic (+128\%).
\item \textbf{Peak-at-\giii{} (V3~\S5.1, Table~\ref{tab:main})}:
\emph{revised}. Under actual pressure, \gv{} is the peak (+11.20
stochastic, +2.90 deterministic). The V3 peak-at-\giii{} result is a
property of the previous low-pressure regime; see
\texttt{docs/v3\_1\_preflight\_findings.md} for the mechanism.
\item \textbf{Deterministic improves monotonically (V3~\S5.3)}:
still true. V3.1 deterministic climbs
\gii{} $-$1.90 $\to$ \gv{} $+$2.90, and the sign flip from negative
(\gii{}) to strongly positive (\gv{}) is sharper than V3's
0.80 $\to$ 2.60 trajectory.
\item \textbf{Knight passivity under locks (V3~\S5.2)}: \emph{partially
revised}. At the V3 lock radius (0.15$W$) knights were near-silent
(0.0 kills/ep). At the V3.1 radius (0.25$W$) knights carry
0.1--1.0 kills/ep across \giii--\gv{}, though \giv{} with its tighter
role constraint still drops to 0.1. The qualitative claim ``tight locks
cause knight passivity'' survives; the specific radius threshold moves.
\item \textbf{Zero failures (V3~\S5.4)}: \emph{obsolete}. V3.1 has
1.8--3.0 failures/episode across all seven games, and
\texttt{-failure\_penalty $\times$ failures\_this\_step} is now part of
the training gradient. The new observation is that \gv{} minimises
failures (1.8) while simultaneously maximising score, i.e.\ the
pragmatic override is performing genuine offense/defense trade-off.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Sensitivity: V3 vs V3.1 as a controlled pressure sweep.}
Because V3.1 changes only the detector and six pressure defaults,
the V3 artifact set (\texttt{models/v3/}, \texttt{results/v3/}) can be
read as an additional datapoint in a spawn-rate / lock-radius sweep.
The qualitative ordering is preserved (G0 baseline ceiling, G2 learned
floor, monotonic structural gain), but under V3's easy regime the
pragmatic override (G5) adds no measurable value, while under V3.1's
realistic regime it contributes the single largest step
(+3.80 stochastic \giv{}$\to$\gv{}). This is consistent with the
primitive's design: the override exists to intervene \emph{when failure
is imminent}, so it is silent when failures never happen.
